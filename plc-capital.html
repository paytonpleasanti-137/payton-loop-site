Compound Annual Growth Rate — strips out volatility to reveal the true annual growth rate of any metric: revenue, earnings, stock price, or portfolio value.
Discounted Cash Flow — intrinsic value is the present value of future cash flows. This is the only valuation method Buffett endorses. "Intrinsic value can be defined simply: it is the discounted value of the cash that can be taken out of a business during its remaining life."

Grammar — What rates are
Interest rates are the price of money over time. The Federal Reserve sets the Fed Funds Rate — the overnight rate at which banks lend to each other. All other rates (mortgage, auto, savings) are priced relative to this benchmark plus a risk spread reflecting the lender’s assessment of default probability and duration.
Logic — Why they matter
Rates are the gravitational force of finance. When rates rise, the present value of all future cash flows falls — assets reprice downward. A 30-year mortgage at 7% vs 3% costs $1,000+ more per month on the same $400k home. Rates determine what you can afford, what businesses can finance, and what your savings actually earn in real terms after inflation.
Rhetoric — The Move
When the Fed Funds Rate is above the 10-year Treasury yield (inverted yield curve), a recession has followed within 12–18 months in every modern cycle. When mortgage rates fall 1%+ below their 12-month high, refinancing applications spike within 60 days. Watch the spread between what you pay on debt and what your assets earn. That gap is your real financial position.

01
Return on Invested Capital
The ultimate engine of wealth creation
—
▼
Grammar — What it is
ROIC is the percentage of cash profit generated for every dollar tied up in operations. Formula: NOPAT ÷ Invested Capital. Invested capital = total debt + total equity − excess cash. It measures how efficiently the business converts capital into profit — independent of how that capital was financed.
Logic — Why it matters
Over decades, a stock's return mirrors the business's ROIC. Growth without high ROIC destroys value — it consumes cash to expand a low-return operation. Munger demanded 15–20%+ sustained ROIC. At 20% ROIC, $1 of reinvested capital becomes $6.19 in 10 years. At 8% ROIC, that same dollar becomes $2.16. The difference is not a rounding error. It is the entire game.
Rhetoric — The Filter
Munger's threshold: 15% minimum, 20%+ preferred. Below 15%: the business is destroying real economic value even if it reports accounting profits. Ask: is this ROIC durable (moat-protected) or cyclical (temporary pricing power)? A company with 25% ROIC for one year is interesting. The same company with 20%+ ROIC for a decade is extraordinary.

02
Owner's Earnings
True free cash flow — what survives the meat grinder of reality
—
▼
Grammar — What it is
Owner's Earnings = Net Income + D&A − Maintenance CapEx. This is the actual cash you could extract from the business without degrading its operations or competitive position. Maintenance CapEx is the mandatory spend just to hold position — not growth investment. The distinction is critical and companies rarely disclose it explicitly.
Logic — Why it matters
Munger called EBITDA "bullshit earnings." Standard accounting spreads asset costs over time on paper, so Wall Street uses EBITDA to measure profitability while ignoring real cash outflows. Physical machines break and technology becomes obsolete. That replacement cash leaves the bank account. It cannot be paid to owners. Owner's Earnings strips the accounting fiction to reveal what actually survives.
Rhetoric — The Filter
Compare Owner's Earnings to reported Net Income. If OE < 60% of Net Income, the business is a capital-consumption machine disguised as a profitable company. Great businesses (See's Candies, Coca-Cola) have OE that exceeds Net Income in good years because D&A overstates real maintenance requirements. That gap is the tell.

03
Margin of Safety
Buying a dollar for 60 cents
—
▼
Grammar — What it is
Intrinsic Value = Owner's Earnings ÷ (Discount Rate − Growth Rate). This is the present value of all future Owner's Earnings discounted back to today. Margin of Safety = the gap between intrinsic value and what the market is asking you to pay. Graham's rule: demand at least a 30% discount. Munger: in extraordinary businesses, a 15–20% discount is sufficient because the moat reduces estimation error.
Logic — Why it matters
Human beings are structurally bad at forecasting. Munger knew he would be wrong about management, the industry, the economy. The margin of safety is the mathematical buffer that absorbs error. Markets are driven by fear and greed. Panic periodically detaches price from value. That gap is not a problem — it is the entire opportunity. The margin of safety exists precisely because crowd psychology creates temporary mispricings in permanent businesses.
Rhetoric — The Filter
Enter the market price and calculate implied Owner's Earnings yield. If the market is paying 40x Owner's Earnings for a 10% grower, there is zero margin of safety — the price already embeds the optimistic scenario. The filter: only act when the market has handed you a structurally protected entry price. Do not negotiate with yourself about whether the business is good enough to overpay for.

04
Debt-to-Equity Hard Limit
Leverage makes a good business risky and a mediocre one lethal
—
▼
Grammar — What it is
Debt-to-Equity = Total Long-Term Debt ÷ Total Shareholders' Equity. Munger's binary rule: D/E below 0.5 passes, above 1.0 fails immediately. No exceptions, no industry adjustments, no "but the cash flows cover it." Debt introduces the mathematical possibility of a zero. Munger refused to allow a zero into his equations regardless of the underlying business quality.
Logic — Why it matters
Management borrows to artificially boost short-term ROE and trigger bonuses. Debt creates fixed, unavoidable interest payments that raise the break-even permanently. Recessions are not optional — revenue eventually drops. Revenue drops while debt payments do not. The business defaults and shareholders are zeroed. Munger's entire framework assumed he would hold through recessions. Leverage made that impossible.
Rhetoric — The Filter
D/E < 0.5: Pass. The business funds itself from earnings. D/E 0.5–1.0: Caution. Understand the debt maturity schedule and covenant structure before proceeding. D/E > 1.0: Stop. Do not rationalize. Munger did not rationalize. The filter is binary because the failure mode is binary: you either survive the downturn or you don't.
